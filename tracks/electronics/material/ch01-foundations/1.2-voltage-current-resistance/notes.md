## §1.1 Introduction

The game of electronics is making and using gadgets that have interesting and useful I-versus-V characteristics. The book begins with the laws, rules of thumb, and tricks that constitute the art of electronics — voltage, current, power, and the components that make up electronic circuits.

An important note on abstraction: you can't touch, see, smell, or hear electricity. The oscilloscope is the most useful visualization tool an electronics engineer has (see Appendix O). Voltmeters are also essential. You will depend on these instruments.

---

## 1.2.1 Voltage and Current

### Voltage

**What it is:** The cost in energy (work done) required to move a unit of positive charge from the more negative point (lower potential) to the more positive point (higher potential). Equivalently, the energy released when a unit charge moves "downhill" from higher to lower potential. Also called *potential difference* or *electromotive force* (EMF). Symbol V (sometimes E).

**Units:** volts. Common scaled units: kV (10^3 V), mV (10^-3 V), μV (10^-6 V). A joule of work is done moving one coulomb through a potential difference of 1 V. One coulomb is approximately 6×10^18 electrons of charge. Nanovolts and megavolts are rare in practice.

**Intuition:** Voltage is always measured *between two points* — never at one point in isolation. When engineers say "the voltage at point X," they mean the voltage between point X and ground (the common reference point everyone agrees on).

### Current

**What it is:** The rate of flow of electric charge past a point. Symbol I.

**Units:** amperes (A = coulombs/second). Common scaled units: mA (10^-3 A), μA (10^-6 A), nA (10^-9 A), pA (10^-12 A).

**Convention:** By convention, current in a circuit flows from a more positive point to a more negative point — even though electrons actually move the other way. Don't call current "amperage" — that's bush league.

**Critical language rule:** Currents flow *through* things. Voltages are applied (or appear) *across* things. "The voltage through a resistor" is nonsense. Always: current *through*, voltage *across* (or *between*).

### How voltages and currents are generated

Voltages are generated by doing work on charges — in batteries (electrochemical energy), generators (mechanical energy), solar cells (photovoltaic conversion of photon energy). Currents are obtained by placing voltages across things.

### The three rules (Kirchhoff's Laws + Power)

**Rule 1 — KCL (conservation of charge):**
```
Sum of currents into a node = sum of currents out
```
For a series circuit (components connected end-to-end), the current is the same everywhere. Engineers call a connection point a *node*.

**Rule 2 — KVL (conservation of energy):**
```
Sum of voltage drops around any closed loop = 0
```
Things hooked in parallel (Figure 1.1) have the same voltage across them. The sum of voltage drops from A to B via any path equals the voltage between A and B. Equivalently, the sum of voltage drops around any closed circuit is zero.

**Rule 3 — Power:**
```
P = VI    (equation 1.1)
```
V in volts, I in amps → P in watts. 1 W = 1 J/s. Example: a 60 W lightbulb on 120 V draws 0.5 A. Power goes to heat (usually), or sometimes mechanical work (motors), radiated energy (lamps, transmitters), or stored energy (batteries, capacitors, inductors).

> **Embedded tie-in:** Managing heat load in a system — even a microcontroller burning milliwatts — requires understanding P = VI. A power-hungry MCU peripheral at 3.3 V drawing 200 mA dissipates 660 mW. That heat has to go somewhere.

**Figure 1.1** shows a parallel connection — the canonical illustration of KVL applied to parallel branches.

## 1.2.2 Relationship between Voltage and Current: Resistors

### A. Resistance and Ohm's Law

**What it is:** For metallic conductors (and other partially conducting materials), the current through the material is proportional to the voltage across it. (Not universal — neon bulbs, diodes, transistors don't obey this.) A *resistor* is characterized by:

**The math:**
```
R = V / I    (equation 1.2)
```
R is in ohms (Ω) for V in volts and I in amps. This is Ohm's law.

**Intuition:** A component is *ohmic* if V/I is constant regardless of V or I. Resistors are ohmic by definition. Diodes, transistors, lightbulbs — not ohmic. The I–V curve for a resistor is a straight line through the origin (Figure 1.15A shows this).

**Physical construction:** A resistor is made of conducting stuff (carbon, thin metal or carbon film, wire of poor conductivity) with a wire or contact at each end. Typical metal-oxide film, metal film, or carbon film resistors range from 1 Ω to 10 MΩ. Also characterized by power rating (most commonly 1/4 W or 1/8 W), tolerance (5%, 2%, 1%), temperature coefficient, noise, and voltage coefficient. Figure 1.2 shows a collection of common resistor packages. Figure 1.3 shows the schematic symbol.

**The 1% standard value series** has 96 values per decade. The 2% and 5% types have 48 and 24 values per decade respectively (see Appendix C).

**Don't call resistance "ohmage."** The preferred term is *resistance*.

> **Embedded tie-in:** Current-limiting resistors on LED outputs, pull-up/pull-down resistors on GPIO lines, and series protection resistors on ADC inputs are all applications of Ohm's law you will encounter on every MCU schematic.

### B. Resistors in Series and Parallel

**The math:**
```
Series (Figure 1.4):
  R = R1 + R2    (equation 1.3)

Parallel (Figure 1.5):
  R = (R1 * R2) / (R1 + R2)    or    R = 1 / (1/R1 + 1/R2)    (equation 1.4)

General parallel (Exercise 1.4):
  R = 1 / (1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...)    (equation 1.5)
```

**Intuition:**
- Series: you always get a *larger* resistor.
- Parallel: you always get a *smaller* resistor.
- Conductance G = 1/R (unit: siemens, S = 1/Ω, also called "mho" — ohm spelled backward). For parallel resistors, conductances simply add: G = G1 + G2 + G3 + ...  This is why the parallel formula looks the way it does.

**Shortcuts the book explicitly gives:**
- Shortcut 1: A large resistor in series (parallel) with a small resistor ≈ the large (small) one. Use this to trim values — add a small series resistor to nudge a value up, a large parallel resistor to nudge it down.
- Shortcut 2: n equal resistors in parallel = R/n. So 5k in parallel with 10k → think of it as two 10k's in parallel (= 5k) in parallel with another 10k → 10k/3 = 3.33k.

**Book exercises on this section:**
- Exercise 1.1: 5k and 10k in series and in parallel.
- Exercise 1.2: 1 Ω across a 12 V car battery — how much power?
- Exercise 1.3: Prove the series and parallel formulas.
- Exercise 1.4: Prove the general parallel formula (equation 1.5).

**Design philosophy the book emphasizes:** Resist the urge to compute resistor values to many significant figures. Components have ±5% or ±1% tolerances; transistor parameters are known only to a factor of 2. Good circuit design is insensitivity to precise component values. Do approximate calculations in your head — "back-of-the-envelope" designing.

### C. Power in Resistors

**The math:**
```
P = IV = I²R = V²/R
```

The two alternate forms come from substituting V = IR or I = V/R into P = IV.

**Intuition:** All power in a resistor is dissipated as heat (or occasionally light, in a lightbulb). It is never returned to the circuit. Resistor power ratings (1/8 W, 1/4 W, 1/2 W, 1 W, and higher) must not be exceeded.

**Book exercises:**
- Exercise 1.5: Show that a 1/4 W resistor > 1k cannot exceed its rating on a 15 V supply, no matter how it is connected.
- Exercise 1.6: New York City needs ~10^10 W at 115 V. What happens if you try to deliver it through a 1-foot-diameter copper cable? (The answer is preposterous — the point is intuition about I²R losses.)

### D. Input and Output — The Transfer Function

Nearly all electronic circuits accept an input (usually a voltage) and produce an output (usually a voltage). Engineers speak of the *transfer function* H — the ratio of output divided by input. For an audio amplifier with gain 100, H = 100. With only resistors, the most important circuit fragment is the voltage divider — a "de-amplifier."

## 1.2.3 Voltage Dividers

### The Circuit

**What it is:** One of the most widespread electronic circuit fragments. Two resistors in series from V_in to ground; output taken from the middle node (Figure 1.6). Given a certain terminal input, it produces a predictable fraction of the input as the output.

**The math:**
```
I = V_in / (R1 + R2)

V_out = I * R2 = V_in * R2 / (R1 + R2)    (equation 1.6)
```

This derivation uses only the series resistor law and Ohm's law. V_out is always less than (or equal to) V_in — hence "divider."

**Intuition:** The output is the fraction of total resistance that sits below the output tap. If R2 is 1/3 of the total, you get 1/3 of V_in. Voltage divides in proportion to resistance.

**Where you see it:** Volume controls (Figure 1.7A shows an adjustable divider with R2 as a variable resistor or potentiometer). Figure 1.7B shows a potentiometer wired as a voltage divider. Figure 1.8 shows common potentiometer styles — panel-mount, board-mount trimmers, optical encoders. ADC input scaling, bias networks, level shifting, sensor bridges.

> **Embedded tie-in:** A pull-up resistor on a GPIO with a button to ground is a voltage divider. The pull-up is R1; the button (near-zero resistance when pressed) is R2. Open: V_out ≈ V_supply. Pressed: V_out ≈ 0 V.

### Loading Effect

The formula above assumes nothing is connected across R2. In real circuits, a load R_load in parallel with R2 changes the effective lower resistance to R2 ‖ R_load, which is always less than R2. Output voltage drops. To minimize this: make R_load >> R2. This undesirable reduction of open-circuit voltage by the load is called *circuit loading*.

The book notes that a voltage divider is "not a very good battery" — its output voltage drops severely when a load is attached. The solution is active voltage sources (op-amps, regulators) covered in later chapters.

## 1.2.4 Voltage Sources and Current Sources

### Voltage Sources

**What it is:** A perfect *voltage source* is a two-terminal black box that maintains a fixed voltage across its terminals regardless of load resistance. It must supply whatever current I = V/R the load demands.

**Real behavior:** A real voltage source behaves like a perfect voltage source with a small series resistance. A standard 9 V alkaline battery ≈ perfect 9 V source in series with 3 Ω. A standard D-size flashlight cell: 1.5 V terminal voltage, ~0.25 Ω series resistance, ~10,000 watt-second energy capacity. At end of life, terminal voltage ≈ 1.0 V with several ohms series resistance.

**Likes and hates:** A voltage source "likes" an open-circuit load (no current to supply) and "hates" a short circuit (demands infinite current). Figure 1.9 shows voltage source symbols — both steady (dc) and varying (ac).

### Current Sources

**What it is:** A perfect *current source* is a two-terminal black box that maintains a constant current through the external circuit regardless of load resistance or applied voltage. It must supply any voltage the load demands.

**Real behavior:** Real current sources have a limit to the voltage they can provide (*output-voltage compliance*, or just *compliance*). They also do not provide absolutely constant output current.

**Likes and hates:** A current source "likes" a short-circuit load and "hates" an open-circuit load. Figure 1.10 shows current source symbols.

**Intuition:** Voltage sources and current sources are exact opposites in their behavior. A battery is a real-life approximation to a voltage source. There is no common analog to a current source (they are constructed from transistors and op-amps, covered in later chapters). Except where portability matters, batteries are rare in electronic devices — most use power supplies.

## 1.2.5 Thévenin Equivalent Circuit

### The Theorem

**What it is:** Thévenin's theorem states that any two-terminal network of resistors and voltage sources is equivalent to a single resistor R in series with a single voltage source V (Figure 1.11). Any mess of batteries and resistors can be mimicked with one battery and one resistor. (Norton's theorem is the dual: any such network is equivalent to a current source in parallel with a resistor.)

**The math:**
```
V_Th = V (open circuit)                         (equation 1.7)
R_Th = V (open circuit) / I (short circuit)
```

**How to find V_Th and R_Th:**
- V_Th: measure (or calculate) the open-circuit voltage at the terminals.
- R_Th: find the short-circuit current; then R_Th = V_Th / I_short. Alternatively, kill all independent sources (short voltage sources, open current sources) and find the resistance looking into the terminals.

### Thévenin of a Voltage Divider

Applying the method to the voltage divider (Figure 1.12):

1. Open-circuit voltage:
```
V_Th = V_in * R2 / (R1 + R2)    (equation 1.8)
```

2. Short-circuit current = V_in / R1

3. Therefore:
```
R_Th = R1 * R2 / (R1 + R2) = R1 ‖ R2    (equation 1.9)
```

It is not a coincidence that R_Th equals the parallel resistance of R1 and R2. The reason will become clear later.

**Example (Figure 1.13):** A 10k–10k voltage divider driven by a perfect 30 V battery is precisely equivalent to a perfect 15 V battery in series with a 5k resistor. Attaching a load causes the output to drop because of this finite source resistance.

**Intuition:** Thévenin tells you two things about any source: what voltage it tries to produce (V_Th) and how "stiff" it is (R_Th). Low R_Th = stiff source — output barely sags under load. High R_Th = soft source — output droops badly when loaded. To avoid loading: R_load >> R_Th (Figure 1.14 shows V_out/V_open vs. R_load/R_out — keep R_load/R_out >> 1).

**Where you see it:**
- Op-amp output: R_Th ≈ milliohms (very stiff).
- GPIO driving HIGH: R_Th ≈ 25–50 Ω.
- 10k–10k voltage divider: R_Th = 5 kΩ — a poor voltage source.

**Book exercises:**
- Exercise 1.10: For the circuit in Figure 1.12 with V_in = 30 V, R1 = R2 = 10k — find (a) open-circuit output voltage, (b) output with 10k load, (c) Thévenin equivalent, (d) repeat (b) using the Thévenin equivalent (must agree), (e) power dissipated in each resistor.
- Exercises 1.7, 1.8, 1.9 (in the Multimeters box): VOM loading, meter shunt resistance, DMM leakage measurement — all apply Thévenin analysis to meter circuits.

### B. Power Transfer

**The result:** Power delivered to R_load is maximized when R_load = R_source (= R_Th).

**Why:** If R_load = 0, V_load = 0 → P = 0. If R_load = ∞, I_load = 0 → P = 0. Maximum is somewhere in between. Exercise 1.11 asks you to show (using calculus, or take it on faith) that the maximum occurs at R_load = R_source.

**Important distinction:** Maximum power transfer ≠ maximum efficiency. At matched impedance, half the power is wasted in R_source (50% efficiency). For power delivery (RF transmitters, audio amplifiers driving speakers, transmission lines) you match impedances. For signal fidelity (voltmeters, ADC inputs, oscilloscopes) you want R_load >> R_source to minimize loading — not for power, but to avoid disturbing the signal.

> **Embedded tie-in:** A GPIO output with R_Th ≈ 40 Ω driving a 10k pull-up/pull-down network sees negligible loading. But driving a low-impedance load (e.g., a buzzer at 8 Ω) directly would be a problem — current limiting and output voltage sag both occur.

## 1.2.6 Small-Signal Resistance

### What it is

For nonlinear devices (diodes, transistors, lightbulbs), I is not proportional to V. The ratio V/I is not constant. For these devices, it is useful to know the *slope* of the I–V curve: the ratio of a small change in applied voltage to the resulting change in current through the device. This is called the *small-signal resistance*, *incremental resistance*, or *dynamic resistance*:

**The math:**
```
r_dyn = dV/dI ≈ ΔV/ΔI
```

Figure 1.15 shows the contrast: A. a resistor (linear I–V curve), B. a zener diode (nonlinear, steep curve in reverse breakdown).

### A. Zener Diodes

**What they are:** Zener diodes have the I–V curve shown in Figure 1.15B. They are used to create a constant voltage inside a circuit, done by providing them with a (roughly constant) current derived from a higher voltage in the circuit (Figure 1.16 — the zener regulator circuit).

**The math (zener small-signal analysis):**

In the circuit of Figure 1.16:
```
I = (V_in - V_out) / R

ΔI = (ΔV_in - ΔV_out) / R

ΔV_out = R_dyn * ΔI = R_dyn/R * (ΔV_in - ΔV_out)

→  ΔV_out = (R_dyn / (R + R_dyn)) * ΔV_in
```

This is the voltage-divider equation again — for *changes* in voltage, the zener acts like a resistor equal to its dynamic resistance, forming a voltage divider with the series resistor R.

**Example the book works through:** A zener with R_dyn = 10 Ω at 10 mA, specified voltage 5 V. A 10% change in current (1 mA change):
```
ΔV = R_dyn * ΔI = 10 Ω × 0.001 A = 10 mV
ΔV/V = 0.002 = 0.2%
```
Good voltage regulation. Figure 1.17 shows measured I–V curves for real low-voltage zeners (1N5221–67 series) — they are "pretty dismal" compared to IC voltage references (LM385Z-1.2, LM385Z-2.5). Zeners near 6 V (e.g., 5.6 V 1N5232B or 6.2 V 1N5234B) have much steeper curves and are more useful.

**Practical note:** R_dyn of a zener varies roughly inversely with current. For better regulation, drive the zener from a current source (R_incr = ∞) rather than a resistor. Low-voltage zeners (e.g., 3.3 V) perform poorly; use a two-terminal voltage reference instead (§9.10).

**Where you see it:** Simple voltage references, overvoltage protection, level-shifting, and — in the book's extended example — temperature sensing circuits.

> **Embedded tie-in:** The BJT small-signal emitter resistance r_e = V_T/I_C ≈ 26 mV/I_C at room temperature is the same concept applied to transistors (covered in Ch. 2). Small-signal resistance is the foundation of all transistor amplifier analysis.

## 1.2.7 An Example: "It's Too Hot!"

### The Circuit

**What it is:** A practical gadget (Figure 1.18 — not reproduced in these notes, see the book) that lights a red LED when the room temperature exceeds 30°C (86°F).

**Components used:**
- R4: thermistor (NTC — negative temperature coefficient), resistance decreases ~4%/°C, 10 kΩ at 25°C. This is the temperature sensor.
- R1, R2: a voltage divider that creates a temperature-insensitive reference voltage.
- Comparator (triangular symbol, discussed in §12.3): switches its output based on which input is higher. When point "X" (thermistor divider) is at a lower voltage than point "Y" (reference divider), comparator pulls its output to ground.
- R5: current-limiting resistor for the LED, sized to allow 5 mA: R5 = (5V − 1.6V)/5mA = 680 Ω.
- LED: behaves electrically like a 1.6 V zener diode. Lights when current flows.

**How it works:**
- The thermistor R4 sits in the lower leg of a voltage divider (R3, R4). As temperature rises, R4 decreases, so the voltage at point "X" drops.
- The reference divider R1, R2 sets a fixed voltage at point "Y."
- When it's hotter than 30°C, "X" drops below "Y," comparator output pulls to ground, current flows through R5 and the LED, LED lights.

**Key design insights from the book:**
- The circuit compares *ratios* (ratiometric technique), not absolute voltages — so it is insensitive to the exact power supply voltage. Ratiometric techniques are broadly useful.
- The setpoint can be made adjustable by replacing R2 with a 5k pot in series with a 5k fixed resistor.
- The book mentions adding *hysteresis* (§12.3) to make the comparator decisive rather than chattering at the threshold.

**Ties together:** voltage dividers, Thévenin equivalents, small-signal thinking (thermistor as a varying resistor), Ohm's law for LED current limiting, and the comparator as a practical building block.

**Where you see it:** Temperature sensing on MCU boards and battery management systems (cell temperature monitoring), fan control circuits, thermostat circuits.

> **Embedded tie-in:** An NTC thermistor in a voltage divider feeding an ADC is one of the most common temperature-sensing topologies in embedded systems. The Thévenin resistance of the divider is R_Th = R3 ‖ R4 — this is the source resistance seen by the ADC input. Keep R_Th small relative to the ADC input impedance to avoid loading error.
